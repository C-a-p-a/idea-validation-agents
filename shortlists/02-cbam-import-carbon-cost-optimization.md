## In plain words

CBAM = the EU's new **"carbon tax on imports."** If you import certain heavy goods into the EU — **steel, aluminium, cement, fertiliser, electricity, hydrogen** — you now have to (a) collect data on how much CO₂ was emitted making them, and (b) buy "CBAM certificates" to cover it. The full system with real payments starts **January 2026**. It's brand new, the supplier-data collection is a nightmare, and the certificates cost real money.

We build software that **handles the CBAM paperwork and minimises the certificate cost** — collect supplier emissions data, calculate the liability, and reduce it (use accurate data instead of the punitive default values, plan certificate purchases, flag lower-carbon suppliers).

## Why it's interesting (it fixes the maritime weakness)

- **No closed-industry barrier.** The buyers are normal manufacturers, importers and wholesalers — far easier to reach than the relationship-locked shipping world. → better founder-fit + distribution than maritime.
- **Norwegian angle:** Norway is a big **exporter** of exactly these goods — aluminium (Hydro), fertiliser (Yara), silicon (Elkem). Their EU customers now demand verified emissions data → a natural local wedge ("help Norwegian exporters give their EU buyers CBAM-ready data").
- Same forced + recurring + expensive + AI-buildable shape as maritime.

## Honest caveats (validate before trusting the score)
- **Confirm Norway/EEA scope** — CBAM applies to imports *into the EU*; Norway's own CBAM adoption timeline needs checking. The exporter-data angle works regardless.
- **Competition is filling fast** — Big-4 consultancies, SAP, and CBAM-specific startups are entering. Saturation likely medium and rising.
- Score is a **pre-research estimate** — it has NOT had the full validation the maritime idea got. Run trend-analysis + competitor-mapper + feasibility before committing.
